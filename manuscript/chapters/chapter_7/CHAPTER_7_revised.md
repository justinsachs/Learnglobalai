# Define: Outcomes Must Be Proofs (Not Objectives)

A course says: "By the end of this module, you will **understand** project management principles."

A manager asks: "Can this person run a project?"

The course cannot answer that question.

Because "understand" is not proof. It is a hope.

You cannot observe understanding. You cannot measure it. You cannot defend it. You cannot verify it transferred to the real world.

"Understand" is what we write when we do not know what competence looks like.

This chapter is about how to turn vague learning objectives into **proof statements**—observable, measurable, defensible definitions of competence. It is about how to write rubrics that make assessment consistent, fair, and auditable. And it is about why this is the most important phase of ADAPT.

Because if you cannot define proof, you cannot verify competence. And if you cannot verify competence, you do not have Adaptive Education™. You have content delivery with a certificate.

## The Problem with Learning Objectives

Most learning objectives are written like this:

- "Understand the principles of X"
- "Learn how to Y"
- "Appreciate the importance of Z"
- "Become familiar with W"

These are not objectives. These are **course descriptions**. They describe what the course will cover, not what the learner will be able to do.

Here is the test: Can you observe it?

- Can you observe "understanding"? No.
- Can you observe "learning"? No.
- Can you observe "appreciation"? No.
- Can you observe "familiarity"? No.

If you cannot observe it, you cannot assess it. If you cannot assess it, you cannot prove it. If you cannot prove it, the credential is meaningless.

Research on Bloom's Taxonomy—cited by over 1,783 papers—shows that learning objectives must differentiate between cognitive skill levels and call attention to objectives that require **higher levels of cognitive skills**, not just recall.[^1]

But most objectives do not even reach the level of recall. They describe internal states that cannot be measured.

This is why the second phase of ADAPT is **Define**: turn objectives into proofs.

## What Is a Proof Statement?

A **proof statement** is a learning objective rewritten as observable performance.

The formula is simple:

**"Learner can [ACTION] under [CONDITIONS] to [STANDARD]"**

Let's break that down:

### 1. ACTION (What They Do)

The action must be **observable and measurable**. Not "understand" or "know," but **demonstrate, calculate, design, diagnose, execute, evaluate, create**.

Research on measurable learning objectives is clear: a learning objective must describe what the learner will **know and be able to DO**, not internal states.[^2]

Benjamin Bloom created a taxonomy of **measurable verbs** to help describe and classify observable knowledge, skills, attitudes, behaviors, and abilities.[^3] Verbs like "understand" or "know" are not measurable. Verbs like "demonstrate," "calculate," "design," and "evaluate" are.

**Examples:**

- **Vague:** "Understand troubleshooting procedures"
- **Proof:** "Diagnose system failures using the troubleshooting protocol"

- **Vague:** "Learn Excel functions"
- **Proof:** "Build a financial model using VLOOKUP, pivot tables, and conditional formatting"

- **Vague:** "Appreciate the importance of data security"
- **Proof:** "Identify vulnerabilities in a system and recommend mitigations"

The action is what you will observe and assess.

---

### 2. CONDITIONS (The Context)

Competence is not universal. It is contextual. A learner might be able to perform a task with unlimited time and full documentation, but fail under time pressure or without reference materials.

So the proof statement must specify the **conditions** under which the learner will perform.

**Examples:**

- "...without reference materials"
- "...within 10 minutes"
- "...under simulated field conditions"
- "...given incomplete or ambiguous information"
- "...while managing competing priorities"

The conditions define the **transfer environment**—the real-world context in which the skill will be used.

If the learner will use the skill under pressure, test them under pressure. If they will use it without documentation, test them without documentation. If they will use it in noisy environments, test them in noisy environments.

The conditions are what make the proof realistic.

---

### 3. STANDARD (The Bar)

The standard defines **what "pass" looks like**. It is the threshold that separates competent from incompetent.

**Examples:**

- "...with 90% accuracy"
- "...meeting all safety requirements"
- "...within acceptable error tolerance"
- "...without critical errors"
- "...to the satisfaction of a qualified supervisor"

The standard is what makes the proof defensible. It is the line you can point to when someone asks: "Is this person competent?"

Without a standard, assessment becomes subjective. With a standard, assessment becomes auditable.

---

### Putting It Together

**Vague Objective:** "Understand project management principles"

**Proof Statement:** "Learner can create a project plan (scope, schedule, budget, risk register) for a realistic scenario, without templates, within 60 minutes, meeting all PMI standards."

Now you have something you can observe, assess, and defend.

## The Three Types of Competence: Knowledge, Skill, and Judgment

Not all competencies are the same. Some require **knowledge** (recall and understanding). Some require **skill** (application and execution). Some require **judgment** (analysis, evaluation, and decision-making in ambiguous situations).

Each type requires a different kind of proof.

### 1. Knowledge (Recall and Understanding)

**What it is:** The ability to recall facts, concepts, and procedures.

**How to prove it:** Written or oral assessment that requires explanation, not just recognition.

**Example Proof Statement:** "Learner can explain the steps of the scientific method and identify which step is being violated in a flawed experiment."

**Assessment Method:** Short-answer questions, concept mapping, teach-back (explain it in your own words).

**Failure Mode:** Multiple-choice questions that test recognition, not understanding.

---

### 2. Skill (Application and Execution)

**What it is:** The ability to perform a task or procedure correctly and consistently.

**How to prove it:** Performance task that requires demonstration under realistic conditions.

**Example Proof Statement:** "Learner can execute the equipment startup procedure without errors, within 5 minutes, following all safety protocols."

**Assessment Method:** Practical demonstration, simulation, recorded performance.

**Failure Mode:** Written test that asks "what would you do?" instead of requiring them to do it.

---

### 3. Judgment (Analysis, Evaluation, Decision-Making)

**What it is:** The ability to make sound decisions in ambiguous, novel, or high-stakes situations.

**How to prove it:** Case study, scenario analysis, or field observation that requires reasoning and justification.

**Example Proof Statement:** "Learner can analyze a business case, identify risks, recommend a course of action, and justify the decision using evidence."

**Assessment Method:** Written case analysis, oral defense, supervisor observation in the field.

**Failure Mode:** Asking for the "right answer" when the situation requires weighing trade-offs.

## Bloom's Taxonomy: The Ladder of Cognitive Complexity

Bloom's Taxonomy is a framework that organizes learning objectives by cognitive complexity. It is one of the most cited frameworks in education (over 1,783 citations)[^1] because it provides a clear structure for designing objectives that require higher-order thinking.

The six levels (revised taxonomy):

1. **Remember:** Recall facts and basic concepts
2. **Understand:** Explain ideas or concepts
3. **Apply:** Use information in new situations
4. **Analyze:** Draw connections among ideas
5. **Evaluate:** Justify a decision or course of action
6. **Create:** Produce new or original work

Most training stops at "Remember" or "Understand." Adaptive Education™ requires at least **Apply**, and often **Analyze**, **Evaluate**, or **Create**.

Why? Because competence is not recall. Competence is **application in context**.

Research shows that Bloom's taxonomy-oriented learning activities significantly improve students' **metacognitive abilities** and strengths in studying and utilizing learning materials.[^4]

**Example:**

- **Remember:** "List the steps in the troubleshooting protocol"
- **Understand:** "Explain why each step is necessary"
- **Apply:** "Use the protocol to diagnose a malfunctioning system"
- **Analyze:** "Identify which step was skipped in a failed troubleshooting attempt"
- **Evaluate:** "Determine whether the protocol is appropriate for this type of failure"
- **Create:** "Design a new troubleshooting protocol for a novel system"

The higher you go on Bloom's Taxonomy, a the closer you get to real-world competence.

## The Rubric: Making Assessment Consistent and Defensible

A proof statement tells you **what** to assess. A rubric tells you **how** to assess it.

A rubric is a scoring guide that defines:

- **What "pass" looks like** (pass threshold)
- **What "fail" looks like** (critical errors)
- **What "mastery" looks like** (excellence indicators)

Without a rubric, assessment is subjective. With a rubric, assessment is **auditable**.

Research is unambiguous: rubrics improve both **validity** (measuring what you intend to measure) and **reliability** (consistency across raters and time).[^5] A systematic review of 75 studies found that reliable scoring of performance assessments can be enhanced by the use of rubrics, improving both inter-rater reliability (agreement between different scorers) and intra-rater reliability (consistency within the same scorer over time).[^6]

In clinical oral assessments, rubric-based assessment showed **high statistical significance (P < 0.0001)** compared to traditional methods.[^7]

The message is clear: **if you want defensible credentials, you need rubrics**.

---

## Anatomy of a Good Rubric

A good rubric has three components:

### 1. Critical Errors (Automatic Fail)

These are mistakes that, if made, mean the learner is not competent—regardless of what else they did correctly.

**Examples:**

- **Safety violation:** Skipped a required safety check
- **Logical error:** Drew a conclusion not supported by evidence
- **Procedural failure:** Skipped a mandatory step
- **Ethical breach:** Violated confidentiality or professional standards

Critical errors are **binary**: you either made the error or you did not. If you made it, you fail.

This is what makes the rubric defensible.

### 2. Pass Threshold (The Bar)

This is the minimum standard for competence. It is a checklist of what the learner must do to pass.

**Examples:**

- "All steps in the procedure were completed in the correct order"
- "The final output meets all specifications"
- "The analysis considers all relevant factors"
- "The communication is clear, concise, and professional"

If the learner meets the pass threshold and has no critical errors, they are competent.

### 3. Excellence Indicators (Mastery)

These are the signs of excellence that separate competence from mastery.

**Examples:**

- **Efficiency:** Completed the task in less time than expected
- **Elegance:** Found a simpler or more creative solution
- **Insight:** Identified a deeper issue or opportunity
- **Leadership:** Helped others to improve their performance

Excellence indicators are not required for competence, but they are what you look for when you are identifying top talent.

## Failure Modes

- **Vague objectives:** Using words like "understand" or "learn" that cannot be measured.
- **Unrealistic conditions:** Testing learners in a sterile environment that does not match the real world.
- **Subjective standards:** Relying on gut feelings instead of a clear rubric.
- **Ignoring Bloom's Taxonomy:** Focusing on recall instead of higher-order thinking.

## Operator Playbook

1. **Audit your learning objectives:** For one of your modules, review the learning objectives. Are they written as proof statements? Can you observe them? Can you measure them?
2. **Rewrite one objective as a proof statement:** Take one of your vague objectives and rewrite it using the formula: "Learner can [ACTION] under [CONDITIONS] to [STANDARD]"
3. **Design a rubric:** For that proof statement, design a rubric with critical errors, a pass threshold, and excellence indicators.
4. **Assess your assessments:** Do your assessments measure knowledge, skill, or judgment? Are they aligned with the cognitive complexity of the task?

## Proof Task

Take a skill you want to learn. Now, write a proof statement for that skill. What is the observable action? What are the conditions? What is the standard?

Now, design a rubric for that proof statement. What are the critical errors? What is the pass threshold? What are the excellence indicators?

This exercise will force you to think like an instructional designer who is building a defensible credential.

## The Steelman Case Against Proofs and Rubrics

The case for proofs and rubrics seems unassailable. They bring clarity, consistency, and defensibility to assessment. But what if they also bring a kind of bureaucratic rigidity that stifles the very learning we are trying to foster? Here are the strongest arguments against the Define phase:

**1. The Argument from the Nature of Expertise:**

Expertise is not a checklist. It is a fluid, intuitive, and often tacit form of knowledge that is difficult to capture in a rubric. A rubric can tell you if a learner has followed the rules, but it cannot tell you if they have developed the kind of deep, intuitive understanding that is the hallmark of true expertise. In this view, an over-reliance on rubrics can lead to a form of "teaching to the test" that produces competent but uninspired performers. It can create a system that is good at producing rule-followers, but not at producing innovators.

**2. The Argument from the Importance of Serendipity and Exploration:**

Learning is not always a linear path to a predefined outcome. Sometimes, the most important learning happens when we are exploring, when we are making mistakes, when we are following our curiosity down a rabbit hole. A system that is too focused on predefined proofs and rubrics can discourage this kind of open-ended exploration. It can create a learning environment that is efficient but sterile, a place where there is no room for serendipity or surprise. In this view, the goal of education is not just to produce competence, but to cultivate curiosity, and a system that is too rigid can get in the way.

**3. The Argument from the Professional Judgment of the Teacher:**

A good teacher is not a machine for applying rubrics. A good teacher is a mentor, a coach, and a guide. They have a deep, intuitive understanding of their students and their subject matter. They can see things that a rubric cannot. They can provide the kind of nuanced, personalized feedback that is essential for deep learning. In this view, an over-reliance on rubrics can devalue the professional judgment of teachers. It can turn them into cogs in a machine, rather than the heart of the educational process.

**4. The Argument from the Risk of Goodhart's Law:**

Goodhart's Law states that "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." When we become too focused on the metrics in a rubric, we can lose sight of the underlying goal. We can start to optimize for the rubric, rather than for learning. This can lead to a kind of gaming of the system, where learners (and teachers) figure out how to get a good score without actually developing the underlying competence. In this view, the very act of measuring can distort the thing we are trying to measure.

These are not reasons to abandon proofs and rubrics. They are reasons to use them wisely. A good proof statement should be a guide, not a straitjacket. A good rubric should be a tool for feedback, not a weapon for judgment. And a good teacher should be empowered to use their professional judgment to go beyond the rubric, to see the whole learner, and to provide the kind of personalized guidance that is the hallmark of great teaching.



## The American Board of Surgery: A Case Study in High-Stakes Competency-Based Assessment

The American Board of Surgery (ABS) is the national certifying body for surgeons in the United States. Its mission is to protect the public by assessing and certifying surgeons who have met the highest standards of education, training, and knowledge. The ABS is a world leader in high-stakes, competency-based assessment, and its approach to the Define phase provides a powerful model for any organization that is serious about verifying competence.

**The Challenge:**

Certifying a surgeon is one of the highest-stakes assessments in the world. A surgeon who is not competent can cause irreparable harm. The ABS needed a way to assess not just what a surgeon knows, but what they can do. It needed a way to verify that a surgeon has the knowledge, skill, and judgment to practice safely and effectively.

**The Solution: A Multi-Faceted, Competency-Based Assessment**

The ABS uses a multi-faceted, competency-based assessment to certify surgeons. The assessment includes:

1.  **The Qualifying Examination (QE):** A written, multiple-choice exam that assesses the surgeon's knowledge of the basic and clinical sciences of surgery.

2.  **The Certifying Examination (CE):** An oral exam that assesses the surgeon's clinical judgment and decision-making skills. The CE is a high-stakes, high-pressure exam that is designed to simulate the real-world challenges of surgical practice.

3.  **The ABS Continuous Certification Program:** A program of lifelong learning and assessment that requires surgeons to demonstrate their continued competence throughout their careers.

The ABS's approach to the Define phase is a masterclass in competency-based assessment. Here's what they do well:

-   **They Define Competence Clearly:** The ABS has a detailed, publicly available blueprint that defines the competencies a surgeon needs to be successful. This blueprint is the foundation for all of their assessments.

-   **They Use a Variety of Assessment Methods:** The ABS understands that no single assessment method can capture the full range of a surgeon's competence. That's why they use a variety of methods, including written exams, oral exams, and a program of continuous certification.

-   **They Focus on Higher-Order Thinking:** The ABS's assessments are not just about what a surgeon knows. They are about what a surgeon can do. The oral exam, in particular, is designed to assess a surgeon's ability to think on their feet, to make sound decisions under pressure, and to justify those decisions with evidence.

-   **They Have a Rigorous, Defensible Process:** The ABS's certification process is rigorous, transparent, and defensible. The exams are developed by a team of experts, and they are constantly reviewed and updated to ensure that they are fair, valid, and reliable.

**The Results:**

The ABS's certification is one of the most respected credentials in the world. It is a powerful signal to patients, hospitals, and the public that a surgeon has met the highest standards of competence.

**The Lessons for Education:**

The ABS's approach to competency-based assessment provides a number of important lessons for education:

-   **High-Stakes Assessment Requires a High-Stakes Process:** If you are going to issue a credential that has real-world consequences, you need to have a rigorous, defensible process for assessing competence.

-   **Competence is Multi-Faceted:** No single assessment method can capture the full range of a learner's competence. A good assessment system will use a variety of methods to assess knowledge, skill, and judgment.

-   **Judgment is the Hardest Thing to Assess, and the Most Important:** The ability to make sound decisions in ambiguous, high-stakes situations is the hallmark of true expertise. A good assessment system will find a way to assess judgment, not just knowledge and skill.

-   **Transparency is Key:** A good assessment system is transparent. The competencies, the assessment methods, and the scoring criteria should all be publicly available. This builds trust and ensures that the system is fair and defensible.

The American Board of Surgery is a powerful example of how the principles of the Define phase can be applied in a high-stakes, real-world setting. It's a reminder that if you are serious about verifying competence, you need to be serious about defining it.
