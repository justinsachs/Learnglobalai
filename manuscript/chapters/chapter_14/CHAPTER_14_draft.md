# Assessment Layer: Integrity by Design

A university moves its final exams online. To prevent cheating, it requires all students to use a remote proctoring service. The software uses the student's webcam and microphone to monitor them for suspicious behavior. It flags students for looking away from the screen, for having another person in the room, for background noise.

A student with ADHD, who needs to look away to focus, is flagged. A student with a child at home, who makes noise in the background, is flagged. A student with a darker skin tone, who is not well-recognized by the facial recognition algorithm, is flagged.

The university thinks it is solving the problem of cheating. Instead, it is creating a new one: a system of surveillance that is biased, invasive, and damages the very trust it is meant to protect.

This is the fundamental failure of the **surveillance-based approach to academic integrity**. It is an arms race that you cannot win, and it comes at a tremendous cost to learner dignity and equity.

The solution is not to build a better mousetrap. The solution is to **design assessments that are inherently resistant to cheating**.

This is the fourth layer of the Adaptive Stack: the **Assessment Layer**, built on the principle of **Integrity by Design**.

This chapter is about why the surveillance model is a dead end, and how to design assessments that promote integrity, not just police it.

## The Problem: Cheating Is Rampant, and Surveillance Is Not the Answer

Let's be clear: cheating in online exams is a massive problem. Studies show that as many as **64% of students admit to some form of online exam cheating**.[^1] One study found that self-reported cheating increased from 30% pre-COVID to 45% during the pandemic.[^2]

The response from many institutions has been to double down on surveillance, deploying remote proctoring software to monitor students during exams.

But this approach is deeply flawed:

### 1. It Is an Invasion of Privacy

These systems are a "‘Big Brother-like’ threat to liberty and privacy."[^3] They turn the student's personal space into a testing center, complete with a remote proctor watching their every move.

### 2. It Is Biased and Inequitable

Algorithmic proctoring systems are notoriously biased. They have been shown to reinforce "white supremacy, sexism, ableism, and transphobia" by flagging non-normative bodies and behaviors as suspicious.[^4]

### 3. It Creates a Climate of Suspicion

Instead of fostering a culture of trust and academic integrity, surveillance-based systems create a climate of suspicion. They treat every student as a potential cheater.

### 4. It Is a Losing Battle

For every new surveillance technique, students will find a new way to cheat. It is an endless and expensive arms race that distracts from the real work of teaching and learning.

As one group of critics put it, "Remote proctoring is not ed tech. It’s **academic surveillance software**."[^5]

It is a solution that is worse than the problem.

---

## The Solution: Integrity by Design

The alternative to the surveillance model is **Integrity by Design**—an approach that focuses on **prevention, not detection**.[^6]

The goal is to design assessments that are so authentic, so tied to the learning process, and so difficult to fake that cheating becomes both harder and less useful than actually learning the material.

Here are the core principles of Integrity by Design:

### 1. Assess Higher-Order Thinking, Not Memorization

As Monash University puts it, "Assessing low level cognitive skills and memorisation lends itself more to cheating."[^7]

If your assessment is a multiple-choice test of facts, you are inviting students to look up the answers. If your assessment is a complex, multi-step project that requires analysis, synthesis, and creation, you are making it much harder to cheat.

**The Rule:** If the answer can be easily Googled, it is a bad question.

### 2. Use Authentic Assessments

Authentic assessments are tasks that mirror the challenges and problems that learners will face in the real world.[^8]

Instead of a multiple-choice test on marketing concepts, have the learner create a marketing plan for a real company.

Instead of a quiz on coding syntax, have the learner build a small application.

It is hard to cheat on a task that requires you to produce a unique, complex artifact that is a direct reflection of your own skills and understanding.

### 3. Assess the Process, Not Just the Product

Instead of a single, high-stakes final exam, assess the learner's progress throughout the learning journey.

- **Scaffold major assignments:** Break large projects into smaller, manageable steps, with check-ins and feedback along the way.
- **Use oral defenses:** Have the learner defend their work in a live conversation. It is much harder to fake understanding when you have to explain your thinking in real-time.
- **Look at the data:** Analyze the learner's process data from the LRS. How did they arrive at their answer? What was their path through the simulation? This can reveal more about their understanding than the final answer itself.

### 4. Design for AI, Don't Ban It

In the age of generative AI, trying to prevent students from using it is a losing battle. Instead, **design assessments that require students to use AI as a tool**.

- **AI as a starting point:** Have students use AI to generate a first draft, and then assess their ability to critique, edit, and improve it.
- **AI as a sparring partner:** Have students debate a topic with an AI, and then assess the quality of their arguments.
- **AI as a data source:** Have students use AI to analyze a dataset, and then assess their ability to interpret the results and draw conclusions.

The goal is not to catch students using AI. The goal is to teach them how to use it effectively, ethically, and responsibly.

---

## The Adaptive Stack: A Multi-Layered Defense

Integrity by Design is not a single solution. It is a multi-layered defense that is woven throughout the Adaptive Stack:

- **Tutor Layer:** The AI tutor can ask probing questions in real-time to check for understanding. "That's an interesting answer. Can you explain how you got there?"

- **Assessment Layer:** Assessments are authentic, performance-based, and assess higher-order thinking.

- **Orchestration Layer:** The Content Factory can generate unique assessment variations for each learner, making it impossible to copy answers.

- **Trust Layer:** The Competence Ledger provides an auditable record of all verified skills, backed by a portfolio of evidence from the LRS.

When a learner claims a competence, you don't just see a badge. You see the evidence: the simulation they passed, the project they completed, the oral defense they gave.

This is what makes the credential defensible. It is not just a claim; it is a **claim backed by proof**.

---

## Failure Modes

- **The Surveillance Arms Race** — trying to build a better mousetrap to catch cheaters
- **Inauthentic Assessments** — assessing memorization instead of application
- **High-Stakes, Single-Event Exams** — creating a high-pressure environment that encourages cheating
- **Banning AI** — an unrealistic and ultimately futile strategy
- **Ignoring the Culture** — failing to build a culture of trust and academic integrity

## Operator Playbook: Redesign One Assessment for Integrity

**Step 1: Pick one assessment from your current program**

**Step 2: Analyze its vulnerability to cheating**
- How easy would it be to cheat on this assessment?
- What tools or techniques could a learner use?

**Step 3: Redesign it using the principles of Integrity by Design**
- How can you shift the focus from memorization to higher-order thinking?
- How can you make it more authentic?
- How can you assess the process, not just the product?
- How can you incorporate AI as a tool?

**Step 4: Define the evidence artifact**
- What will the learner produce to prove competence?
- How is this different from the original assessment?

**Step 5: Document the new assessment design**
- What are the benefits of this new design?
- What are the challenges in implementing it?

---

## Proof Task

Think about the last time you were tempted to "cheat" on a learning task (e.g., looking up the answer instead of figuring it out, skipping a reading, using a shortcut).

**Step 1: Analyze the assessment**
- What was the task?
- Why were you tempted to cheat?
- Was it because the task was inauthentic? Boring? Too hard? Too easy?

**Step 2: Redesign the task**
- How could you redesign the task to make it more engaging and less "cheatable"?
- What would make you *want* to do the work?

**Step 3: Reflect on the role of motivation**
- How much of academic integrity is about motivation vs. policing?
- How can we design learning experiences that are so intrinsically motivating that cheating becomes irrelevant?
