# Assessment Layer: Integrity by Design

A university moves its exams online, turning student bedrooms into surveillance suites. Remote proctoring software monitors students for suspicious behavior, flagging them for looking away, a child crying, or having a darker skin tone.

A student with ADHD is flagged for looking away. A student with a child is flagged for background noise. A student with a darker skin tone is flagged by a biased algorithm.

The university, trying to solve cheating, creates a new problem: a biased, invasive surveillance system that erodes trust.

This is the failure of the **surveillance-based approach to academic integrity**: a losing battle that costs learner dignity, equity, and trust.

The solution is not a better mousetrap, but **assessments designed to be inherently cheat-resistant**.

This is the **Assessment Layer**, built on **Integrity by Design**.

This chapter explores a better way, moving beyond the surveillance model by rethinking assessment. We'll explore how to design assessments that promote integrity by making learning more valuable than cheating.

## The Problem: Cheating Is Rampant, and Surveillance Is Not the Answer

Academic dishonesty is a significant problem, especially in online learning where opportunities for cheating have multiplied. Studies show a majority of students admit to cheating in online exams, with a dramatic increase during the COVID-19 pandemic.[^1][^2] Many institutions have responded by deploying remote proctoring software.



But this approach is flawed:

**1. It's an Invasion of Privacy:** These systems turn a student's personal space into a testing center, with a remote proctor watching every move. This is a deeply intrusive act of surveillance.[^3]

**2. It's Biased and Inequitable:** Algorithmic proctoring systems are biased, amplifying existing inequalities. They are less accurate with darker skin tones, flag students with disabilities, and reinforce a narrow, normative view of a "good" student, encoding biases into a supposedly neutral system.[^4]

**3. It Creates a Climate of Suspicion:** Academic integrity is built on trust. Surveillance systems start from distrust, creating a toxic, adversarial relationship that undermines the learning community.

**4. It's a Losing Battle:** The surveillance arms race is a fool's errand. For every new technique, a new circumvention method emerges. This is not a battle that can be won with technology.

Remote proctoring is **academic surveillance software**, not ed tech.[^5]



---

## The Solution: Integrity by Design

If surveillance is a dead end, the alternative is **Integrity by Design**, a paradigm shift from detection to prevention.[^6] It's a holistic philosophy of assessment design that makes academic integrity the path of least resistance.

The goal is to create assessments so authentic and integrated with the learning process that cheating becomes more difficult and less useful than learning. The incentive structure is aligned with genuine mastery.

Core principles of Integrity by Design:

**1. Assess Higher-Order Thinking, Not Memorization:** Move up the cognitive ladder. Assessments that focus on recall are an invitation to cheat.[^7] A knowledge worker is valued for what they can do, not what they remember.

A multiple-choice test of easily searchable facts assesses search engine skills, not competence. A complex project that requires analysis, synthesis, and creation assesses real-world skills and makes cheating harder and less useful.

**Rule:** If the answer is easily Googled, it's a bad question.

**2. Use Authentic Assessments:** These are tasks that reflect real-world challenges.[^8] They are messy, complex, and ill-defined, like real-world problems.

Instead of a multiple-choice test on marketing, have the learner create a marketing plan.

Instead of a quiz on coding, have the learner build an application.

It's hard to cheat on a task that requires a unique, complex artifact reflecting your own skills. The artifact is the evidence of competence, and its creation is the learning.

**3. Assess the Process, Not Just the Product:** A single, high-stakes final exam encourages cheating. A better approach is to assess progress continuously with frequent, low-stakes feedback opportunities.

- **Scaffold assignments:** Break large projects into smaller steps with feedback.
- **Use oral defenses:** Have the learner defend their work. It's harder to fake understanding when explaining your thinking in real-time.
- **Analyze the data:** The learner's process data from the LRS can reveal more about their understanding than the final answer.

**4. Design for AI, Don't Ban It:** Banning generative AI is a losing battle and a pedagogical mistake. Instead, design assessments that require students to use AI effectively and ethically.

- **AI as a starting point:** Have students use AI for a first draft, then assess their ability to critique and improve it.
- **AI as a sparring partner:** Have students debate an AI, then assess their arguments.
- **AI as a data source:** Have students use AI to analyze a dataset, then assess their interpretation.

The goal is not to catch students using AI, but to teach them to use it effectively, ethically, and responsibly.

## The Adaptive Stack: A Multi-Layered Defense

Integrity by Design is a philosophy woven into every layer of the Adaptive Stack, a multi-layered defense that makes academic dishonesty more difficult and less rewarding.

- **The Tutor Layer as a Real-Time Defense:** The AI tutor, with its Socratic dialogue, acts as a first line of defense against shallow understanding, making it difficult to succeed by regurgitation.

- **The Assessment Layer as the Core of the Strategy:** This is where Integrity by Design is most explicit, with authentic, performance-based assessments focused on higher-order thinking.

- **The Orchestration Layer as a Source of Infinite Variety:** The Content Factory can generate infinite unique assessment variations, making direct copying impossible.

- **The Trust Layer as the Ultimate Verification:** The Competence Ledger is the ultimate backstop, a rich, auditable record of verified skills backed by a portfolio of evidence from the LRS.

When a competence is claimed, employers can access the underlying evidence: the simulation passed, the project completed, the oral defense recording, the full tutor interaction history. This provides a level of transparency impossible in the traditional system.

This makes the credential defensible: a **claim backed by a deep, auditable portfolio of proof**. The system makes cheating irrelevant; the only way to succeed is to do the work, which is the proof of competence.

## Failure Modes

- **The Surveillance Arms Race:** Trying to build a better mousetrap to catch cheaters.
- **Inauthentic Assessments:** Assessing memorization instead of application.
- **High-Stakes, Single-Event Exams:** Creating a high-pressure environment that encourages cheating.
- **Banning AI:** An unrealistic and ultimately futile strategy.
- **Ignoring the Culture:** Failing to build a culture of trust and academic integrity.

## The Steelman Case Against Integrity by Design

The principle of 'Integrity by Design' is an elegant and humane response to the ugly problem of academic dishonesty. But is it a realistic solution for a world that is messy, unequal, and resource-constrained? The strongest counterarguments do not defend surveillance, but rather question the feasibility and fairness of the proposed alternative.

**1. The Argument from Scalability and Cost: Authentic Assessment is a Luxury Good**

The core of Integrity by Design is the shift to authentic, performance-based assessment. But this kind of assessment is incredibly expensive and difficult to scale. It requires highly skilled educators to design the tasks, provide personalized feedback, and grade the complex artifacts that students produce. A multiple-choice test can be graded by a machine in seconds. A portfolio of student work, a complex project, or an oral defense can take hours of an expert's time to evaluate. For large-scale educational institutions, especially those serving underprivileged populations, the cost and logistical complexity of authentic assessment at scale are simply prohibitive. In this view, Integrity by Design is a luxury good, a boutique solution for elite institutions, not a viable model for mass education.

**2. The Argument from Subjectivity and Bias: The Unreliable Nature of Human Judgment**

Authentic assessments are graded by humans, and human judgment is notoriously subjective and prone to bias. A grader's evaluation of a student's portfolio can be influenced by their mood, their personal pedagogical beliefs, and their unconscious biases about the student's race, gender, or background. Standardized tests, for all their flaws, at least offer a veneer of objectivity. By moving away from standardized, machine-gradable assessments towards more subjective, human-graded ones, we are not eliminating bias, but simply trading one form of bias for another. In this view, the move to authentic assessment is a move away from objectivity and fairness, and towards a system where the student's grade is more a reflection of the grader than of their own competence.

**3. The Argument from the Necessity of Foundational Knowledge: You Can't Have Higher-Order Thinking Without Lower-Order Facts**

Integrity by Design places a heavy emphasis on assessing higher-order thinking skills like analysis, synthesis, and creation. But you can't think critically about a subject you know nothing about. Higher-order thinking is built on a foundation of lower-order factual knowledge. While it is easy to dismiss memorization as a low-level skill, it is a necessary precondition for any meaningful intellectual work. An assessment strategy that completely abandons the assessment of foundational knowledge in favor of higher-order thinking is a strategy that is building a house on a foundation of sand. In this view, the dismissal of factual knowledge assessment is a form of pedagogical malpractice that will ultimately harm the very students it is trying to help.

**4. The Argument from the Reality of Human Motivation: Students Will Always Take the Path of Least Resistance**

The Integrity by Design model assumes that if you make assessments authentic and engaging, students will be intrinsically motivated to do the work and will not be tempted to cheat. This is a naive and overly optimistic view of human nature. Students are under immense pressure to succeed, and they will always be tempted to take the path of least resistance. Even the most authentic assessment can be gamed. Students can collaborate inappropriately, they can plagiarize ideas, and they can use AI to do the bulk of the work for them. In this view, while Integrity by Design is a noble ideal, it is not a substitute for a robust system of verification and enforcement. A healthy culture of academic integrity requires both trust and verification, both prevention and detection.

These are not arguments for returning to the surveillance state. They are arguments for a more clear-eyed and pragmatic approach to assessment design. They are a reminder that there are no easy answers, and that every solution comes with its own set of trade-offs and compromises.


## WGU: A Case Study in Assessment with Integrity by Design

Western Governors University (WGU) is a pioneer in competency-based education and a powerful example of an institution that has built its assessment model on the principles of Integrity by Design. Founded in 1997 by a group of U.S. governors, WGU has no classes, no lectures, and no required seat time. Instead, students progress by demonstrating their competence through a series of rigorous assessments. This model, by its very nature, is designed to be resistant to the kinds of cheating that plague traditional, time-based systems.

**The WGU Assessment Model: A Two-Pronged Approach**

WGU uses a two-pronged approach to assessment:

1.  **Objective Assessments (OAs):** These are proctored, multiple-choice exams that are designed to test a student's foundational knowledge. While these may seem like traditional tests, they are used in a very different way. They are not high-stakes, one-shot events. Students can take pre-assessments to gauge their readiness, and they can take the OA as many times as they need to demonstrate mastery. The goal is not to catch students cheating, but to ensure that they have the foundational knowledge they need to succeed in the more complex performance assessments.

2.  **Performance Assessments (PAs):** These are the heart of the WGU model. They are authentic, project-based assessments that require students to apply their knowledge and skills to solve real-world problems. A student in the business program might be asked to develop a marketing plan for a new product. A student in the nursing program might be asked to create a care plan for a patient with a complex set of symptoms. A student in the IT program might be asked to design and build a secure network for a small business. These are not tasks that can be easily outsourced or faked. They require the student to produce a unique, complex artifact that is a direct reflection of their own competence.

**Integrity by Design in Action:**

WGU's assessment model is a masterclass in Integrity by Design:

-   **Authenticity:** The performance assessments are designed to be as authentic as possible, mirroring the kinds of tasks that students will be expected to perform in their future careers. This makes the assessments more engaging and meaningful for students, and it also makes them much harder to cheat on.

-   **Higher-Order Thinking:** The performance assessments are designed to assess higher-order thinking skills like analysis, synthesis, and creation. They are not about memorizing facts; they are about applying knowledge to solve complex problems.

-   **Process and Product:** WGU assesses both the process and the product of learning. The objective assessments ensure that students have the necessary foundational knowledge, and the performance assessments demonstrate their ability to apply that knowledge in a meaningful way.

-   **Human-in-the-Loop:** The performance assessments are graded by a team of highly trained, subject-matter expert evaluators. These evaluators use detailed rubrics to ensure that the grading is fair, accurate, and reliable. This human-in-the-loop approach is a critical part of the WGU model, as it provides a level of nuance and judgment that is not possible with a machine-graded assessment.

**The Results:**

The WGU model has been incredibly successful. The university has grown to over 130,000 students and has been widely recognized for its innovative approach to higher education. But more importantly, it has created a system where the incentive is to learn, not to cheat. When the assessment is the work, and the work is authentic and meaningful, academic integrity is not something that has to be policed; it is something that emerges naturally from the design of the learning experience itself.

WGU is a powerful reminder that the best way to deal with the problem of cheating is not to build a better surveillance system, but to build a better assessment system. It is a reminder that when we design for integrity, we create a system that is not just more secure, but also more engaging, more equitable, and more effective.
