# Universities and Credential Providers: From Credit Hours to Proof Hours

For over a century, the **credit hour** has been the currency of higher education—a measure of time, a proxy for learning, and the foundation of its business model.

Degrees, workloads, and revenue are all based on it.

But the credit hour is a flawed construct with **no relationship to learning**.[^1]

It measures seat time, not competence—a relic of an industrial-era model no longer fit for purpose.

The future is not about credit hours, but **proof hours**—a measure of verified competence backed by evidence.

This chapter is about the *proposed* shift from the time-based economy of the credit hour to the performance-based economy of the proof hour, and its implications for universities and credential providers. We will explore both the powerful arguments for this transition and the significant, real-world regulatory and practical hurdles that stand in the way. The credit hour, for all its flaws, is deeply embedded in the legal and financial infrastructure of higher education, and its displacement will be a complex, political, and generational challenge, not a simple matter of technological substitution.

## The Credit Hour Is Bankrupt

The credit hour was a useful innovation, creating a standardized unit of exchange for credit transfers and a national market for higher education.

But it has outlived its usefulness for several reasons:

**1. It Measures Time, Not Learning:** The credit hour measures input (time), not output (learning), assuming all students learn at the same pace and that learning fits into 15-week semesters.

This is false. Students learn at different rates, and learning isn't linear. The credit hour ignores this.

**2. It Creates Perverse Incentives:** The credit hour incentivizes minimal effort from students and standardized course design from faculty, regardless of student needs.

It also incentivizes institutions to maximize credit hour sales, focusing on enrollment over outcomes.

**3. It Is a Barrier to Innovation:** The entire infrastructure of higher education is built around the credit hour, making innovation difficult.

New, non-time-based models like competency-based education, bootcamps, and apprenticeships are forced to fit into the credit hour box.[^2]

## The Rise of Competency-Based Education (CBE)

**Competency-based education (CBE)** is a pedagogical alternative to the credit hour,[^3] allowing students to **advance based on mastery at their own pace**.[^4]

In a CBE model, the focus is on *what* you learn, not how long.

- **Personalized:** Students accelerate through what they know and spend more time on what they need to learn.
- **Flexible:** Students learn anytime, anywhere.
- **Outcomes-driven:** The goal is mastery of workforce-aligned competencies.

CBE is not new, but it has seen a resurgence due to demand for flexible, affordable, and career-relevant education.[^5]

## From CBE to Proof Hours

CBE is a step in the right direction, but not the final destination.

The next phase is the shift from **competencies** to **proof**.

It's not enough to say a student has mastered a competency; you have to **prove it**.

This is where the **Proof Hour** comes in.

A **Proof Hour** is a unit of verified competence, a claim backed by a portfolio of evidence in their Competence Ledger.

- A **Credit Hour** says, "You sat in a chair for 15 hours."
- A **Proof Hour** says, "You proved you can do X, and here's the evidence."

This is a fundamental shift from a time-based to a performance-based economy in education.

### The Anatomy of a Proof Hour

A Proof Hour is a collection of evidence demonstrating competence, including:

- **Authentic assessments:** Projects, presentations, simulations.
- **Third-party validations:** From supervisors, mentors, or peers.
- **A collection of work:** Portfolios, GitHub repositories, blog posts.

Each piece of evidence is an xAPI statement in the LRS, linked to a competence in the Competence Ledger.

This makes the Proof Hour powerful: it's a **defensible claim**.

## The Implications for Universities and Credential Providers

The shift from credit hours to proof hours will profoundly impact all of higher education:

**1. Business Model:** Universities will shift from selling time to **verifying competence**.

Revenue will be based on helping students acquire and prove valuable skills, not on enrollment numbers.

This will force a greater focus on outcomes and workforce alignment.

**2. Pedagogy:** The focus will shift from **delivering content** to **designing experiences** for learners to practice and demonstrate competence.

Faculty will become more like **coaches and mentors**, guiding learners and helping them build their proof portfolios.

**3. Credentials:** Degrees and certificates will become less important than the **underlying portfolio of proof**.

An employer will see not just a marketing degree, but the marketing plans, campaigns, and data that prove the candidate's effectiveness.

This is the shift from **courses and grades to skills and evidence**.[^6]

## The Role of Adaptive Education™

Adaptive Education™ is the operating system for the proof-hour economy.

- **ADAPT Framework:** Provides the methodology for designing learning experiences that lead to proof.
- **Adaptive Stack:** Provides the technology for verifying competence at scale and managing the Competence Ledger.
- **Adaptive Learning Flywheel:** Creates a virtuous cycle where the system improves at verifying competence with each learner.

Universities and credential providers that embrace this new model will thrive; those that cling to the credit hour will be left behind.

- **Focusing on completion over competence:** Issuing credentials without proof.
- **Ignoring employer needs:** Creating programs not aligned with relevant skills.
- **Failing to build infrastructure:** Trying to move to a proof-based model without the Adaptive Stack.

## The Steelman Case for the Credit Hour

The idea of the "proof hour" is a seductive one. It speaks to our desire for a true meritocracy, a world where the only thing that matters is what you can do, not where you went to school or how long you sat in a classroom. But the strongest argument for the credit hour is not that it is a perfect measure of learning, but that it is a flawed but necessary tool for managing the complex social, political, and economic realities of higher education. The strongest argument against the proof hour is not that it is a bad idea, but that it is a utopian fantasy that would collapse under the weight of its own good intentions.

**1. The Argument from Practicality and Standardization: The Credit Hour as a Necessary Evil**

The credit hour is the lingua franca of higher education. It is a simple, standardized, and universally understood unit of exchange. It is what allows a student to transfer from a community college in California to a four-year university in New York. It is what allows the federal government to disburse billions of dollars in financial aid. It is what allows a university to manage the workload of thousands of faculty members. Is it a perfect measure of learning? No. But it is a good enough measure, and it is the only measure that works at scale. The proof hour, in contrast, is a chaotic and unstandardized mess. Every institution would have its own definition of "proof," its own portfolio of evidence, its own idiosyncratic system of verification. The result would not be a true meritocracy, but a Tower of Babel, a world where credentials are even more opaque and untrustworthy than they are today. In this view, the credit hour is not a relic of the industrial age, but a brilliant and enduring social technology, a necessary evil that makes the entire system work.

**2. The Argument from the Unmeasurable Nature of True Learning: The Proof Hour as a Reductionist Trap**

The proof hour is based on the assumption that all learning can be reduced to a set of discrete, measurable, and verifiable competencies. But the most important outcomes of a true education are not measurable. They are the habits of mind, the intellectual virtues, the capacity for critical thinking, the love of learning, the ability to grapple with ambiguity and complexity. These are the things that make us human, and they cannot be captured in a portfolio of evidence. The proof hour, in its relentless focus on the measurable, would inevitably lead to a curriculum that is optimized for the measurable. It would be a world of vocational training, a world where the liberal arts are a luxury and the humanities are a hobby. In this view, the proof hour is not a more authentic measure of learning, but a more impoverished one, a reductionist trap that would drain the soul from higher education.

**3. The Argument from Equity and Access: The Proof Hour as a Tool of Privilege**

The proof hour is presented as a tool for equity, a way to level the playing field and allow anyone to prove their skills, regardless of their background. But the reality is that the ability to produce a polished portfolio of "proof" is itself a skill, and it is a skill that is highly correlated with socioeconomic status. The student from a wealthy family, with access to private tutors, and a network of well-connected mentors, will always be better at producing "proof" than the student from a poor family who is working two jobs to make ends meet. The proof hour would not be a great equalizer; it would be a great amplifier of existing inequalities. In this view, the credit hour, for all its flaws, is a more equitable system. It is a simple, transparent, and universal standard that applies to everyone, regardless of their background. It is a system that is based on the democratic principle that everyone deserves a chance to learn, not just those who are good at marketing themselves.

**4. The Argument from the Social Fabric of Learning: The Proof Hour as an Atomizing Force**

The proof hour is a deeply individualistic concept. It is about what *you* can prove, what *you* have mastered, what *you* have in your portfolio. But learning is not an individual sport. It is a social activity. It is about the relationships between students and faculty, the debates in the classroom, the late-night conversations in the dorm room. It is about the serendipitous encounters, the unexpected collaborations, the shared struggle of learning together. The proof hour, in its focus on the individual, would atomize the learning experience. It would turn the university from a community into a marketplace, a place where individuals come to acquire skills, not to be transformed by a shared intellectual journey. In this view, the credit hour, by bringing people together in a specific place for a specific period of time, is a powerful force for community, a force that is essential for the cultivation of not just skills, but of citizens.

These are not arguments for complacency. They are arguments for a more sober and realistic view of the challenges of educational reform. They are a reminder that the credit hour is not just a unit of time, but a complex social institution, and that any attempt to replace it must grapple with the full range of its functions, both intended and unintended.


## Western Governors University: A Case Study in Competency at Scale

No discussion of the shift from time to competence is complete without a deep look at Western Governors University (WGU). Founded in 1997 by a group of 19 U.S. governors, WGU was a radical experiment in higher education. Its founding premise was simple but profound: what if we measured learning, not time? What if a degree was not a collection of credit hours, but a demonstration of competence? For over two decades, WGU has been the leading pioneer in competency-based education (CBE), and its success provides a powerful blueprint for the future of higher education.

**The Problem: A System Designed for a Different Era**

The founders of WGU recognized that the traditional, time-based model of higher education was failing to meet the needs of a rapidly changing economy. The one-size-fits-all, lecture-based model was too slow, too expensive, and too disconnected from the skills that employers actually needed. The credit hour, the sacred currency of the academy, was a proxy for learning, not a measure of it. The result was a system that was failing to serve the needs of a large and growing population of adult learners, who needed a more flexible, affordable, and career-relevant path to a credential.

**The Solution: A Purely Competency-Based Model**

WGU's solution was to build a new kind of university from the ground up, a university that was designed around the principles of competency-based education. Here's how it works:

1.  **A Focus on Competencies, Not Courses:** A WGU degree is not a collection of courses; it is a collection of competencies. Each degree program is designed in consultation with industry experts to identify the specific skills and knowledge that a graduate needs to be successful in their chosen field. These competencies are the foundation of the entire curriculum.

2.  **Direct Assessment, Not Seat Time:** Students at WGU do not earn credit for sitting in a classroom. They earn credit for demonstrating their competence through a series of rigorous assessments. These assessments can take many forms, from traditional exams to performance-based tasks, projects, and portfolios. The key is that they are a direct measure of the student's ability to apply their knowledge and skills in a real-world context.

3.  **Personalized Pacing, Not a Fixed Calendar:** Because the focus is on competence, not time, students can move through the curriculum at their own pace. If a student already has a particular skill, they can prove it through an assessment and move on. If they need more time to master a concept, they can take it. This allows for a truly personalized learning journey, one that is tailored to the individual needs of each student.

4.  **A Disaggregated Faculty Model:** WGU's faculty model is also radically different from that of a traditional university. Instead of a single professor who is responsible for everything from lecturing to grading, WGU has a disaggregated faculty model. There are course instructors who are subject matter experts, program mentors who provide one-on-one coaching and support, and assessment faculty who are responsible for evaluating student work. This allows each faculty member to focus on what they do best, and it provides students with a more comprehensive and personalized support system.

**The Results: A Model That Works**

The results of WGU's experiment have been nothing short of remarkable. The university has grown from a small pilot program to a national institution with over 130,000 students. It has been widely recognized for its innovative model, and it has consistently received high marks for student satisfaction and employer approval. WGU has proven that it is possible to deliver a high-quality, affordable, and flexible education at scale, and it has done so by challenging the most fundamental assumptions of traditional higher education.

**The Lesson for the Proof Hour:**

WGU is the living embodiment of the shift from credit hours to proof hours. It is a university that has built its entire model around the principle of verified competence. It is a university that has shown that it is possible to create a more fair, more flexible, and more effective system of higher education, one that is designed for the needs of the 21st-century learner. The success of WGU is a powerful testament to the fact that the future of education is not about how long you sit in a chair, but what you can prove you can do.
