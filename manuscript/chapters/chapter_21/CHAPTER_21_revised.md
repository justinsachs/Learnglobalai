# Credentials the Market Respects: Evidence, Not Badges

In the last decade, the world has been flooded with **digital badges**. They are everywhere. You can get a badge for completing a one-hour online course, for attending a webinar, for being a “team player.”

The promise of digital badges was to create a new, more granular way to signal skills and achievements. The reality has been a **race to the bottom**.

The market is now saturated with low-quality, unverified badges that are confusing to employers and have **very limited value**.[^1]

This is the **problem of credential inflation**. When everyone has a badge for everything, badges mean nothing.

Employers do not want more credentials. They want **reliable signals of competence**. They want **proof**.

This chapter is about why the digital badge revolution has failed to deliver on its promise, and why the future of credentialing belongs to **verifiable, evidence-based credentials** that the market can trust.

## The Failure of the Digital Badge

Digital badges are not inherently bad. The idea of a portable, digital representation of a skill is a good one.

But the implementation has been deeply flawed.

### 1. Lack of Verification

Most digital badges are not verified. They are simply a claim that a learner has completed a task. There is no way for an employer to know if the learner actually has the skill that the badge represents.[^2]

### 2. Lack of Standards

There are no common standards for what a badge represents. A “Data Science” badge from one provider might represent 100 hours of rigorous, project-based work. A “Data Science” badge from another provider might represent a two-hour introductory webinar.

This makes it impossible for employers to compare candidates or to know what a badge actually means.

### 3. The Signal-to-Noise Problem

The proliferation of low-quality badges has created a massive signal-to-noise problem. It is hard for employers to distinguish the meaningful credentials from the meaningless ones.

As a result, many employers have simply given up on badges altogether.

## The Shift to Verifiable, Evidence-Based Credentials

The failure of the digital badge has created a vacuum in the market. And that vacuum is now being filled by a new generation of **verifiable, evidence-based credentials**.[^3]

These are not just claims of competence. They are **defensible claims**, backed by a rich portfolio of evidence.

Here is what makes them different:

### 1. They Are Verifiable

A verifiable credential is a digital credential that is cryptographically secure and can be independently verified by a third party.[^4]

This means that an employer can be sure that the credential is authentic and has not been tampered with.

### 2. They Are Evidence-Based

An evidence-based credential is not just a badge. It is a **link to a portfolio of proof**.[^5]

When an employer views an evidence-based credential, they do not just see the name of the skill. They see the evidence that proves the learner has that skill:

- The authentic assessment they passed
- The project they completed
- The simulation they mastered
- The feedback they received from their mentor

This is the difference between a **claim** and **proof**.

### 3. They Are Aligned with Employer Needs

Evidence-based credentials are not created in a vacuum. They are designed in partnership with employers to ensure that they are aligned with the skills that are actually needed in the workforce.[^6]

This makes them a much more reliable signal of competence than a traditional degree or a generic digital badge.

## The Competence Ledger: The Ultimate Verifiable Credential

The **Competence Ledger** is the ultimate verifiable, evidence-based credential.

It is a secure, auditable, and comprehensive record of everything a learner can do, backed by a portfolio of proof in their Learning Record Store (LRS).

When a learner shares their Competence Ledger with an employer, they are not just sharing a list of skills. They are sharing a **rich, detailed, and verifiable story of their learning journey**.

- The employer can see the specific competencies they have mastered.
- They can see the assessments they have passed.
- They can see the projects they have completed.
- They can even see the raw data from their simulations and performance on the job.

This is a level of transparency and trust that has never been possible before.

It is the end of the resume as we know it. And it is the beginning of a new era of **meritocracy**, where what you can prove you can do is more important than where you went to school or who you know.

---

## Failure Modes

- **Issuing badges without evidence** — contributing to the credential inflation problem
- **Failing to engage employers** — creating credentials that are not aligned with market needs
- **Lack of a secure, verifiable infrastructure** — creating credentials that cannot be trusted
- **Focusing on the credential instead of the competence** — treating the badge as the goal, rather than the learning

## Operator Playbook: Design an Evidence-Based Credential

**Step 1: Pick one skill or competency**

**Step 2: Define the proof**
- What will a learner have to do to prove they have this skill?
- What is the authentic assessment?
- What is the evidence artifact?

**Step 3: Design the credential**
- What will the credential be called?
- What information will it contain?
- How will it link to the underlying evidence?

**Step 4: Engage employers**
- Share your design with employers in your industry.
- Get their feedback. Would this be a valuable signal to them?
- What would make it more valuable?

**Step 5: Build the ecosystem**
- How will you issue the credential?
- How will learners share it?
- How will employers verify it?

## The Steelman Case for the Traditional Degree

The vision of a perfectly meritocratic, evidence-based, and radically transparent labor market is a seductive one. It is a vision of a world where the only thing that matters is what you can prove you can do, a world where the traditional, and often exclusionary, signals of prestige—the elite university, the fancy internship, the well-connected family—are rendered obsolete. But what if this vision is not just a utopian fantasy, but a dystopian one? The strongest arguments for the traditional degree are not that it is a perfect measure of competence, but that it is a rich, multi-faceted, and deeply human signal that captures a range of qualities that are essential for success in the modern economy, qualities that a purely evidence-based credential, in its relentless focus on the measurable, would inevitably miss.

**1. The Argument from the Unmeasurable Nature of a Good Education: The Degree as a Proxy for Intellectual Virtue**

The most important outcomes of a good education are not the specific skills that are learned, but the intellectual virtues that are cultivated. It is the ability to think critically, to write clearly, to speak persuasively, to grapple with ambiguity, to learn how to learn. These are not skills that can be easily measured or neatly packaged into a verifiable credential. They are habits of mind that are forged through years of deep and sustained engagement with a rich intellectual tradition, through the give-and-take of the seminar room, through the mentorship of a great professor. The traditional degree, for all its flaws, is a proxy for this kind of intellectual formation. It is a signal that a person has not just acquired a set of skills, but has been initiated into a community of inquiry, a community that values intellectual curiosity, rigor, and a commitment to the life of the mind. In this view, the obsession with the measurable and the verifiable is a form of reductionism, a form of reductionism that would devalue the very qualities that are most essential for a flourishing intellectual life.

**2. The Argument from the Social Fabric of the University: The Degree as a Signal of Social Capital**

A university is not just a place where knowledge is transmitted. It is a place where social capital is created. It is a place where lifelong friendships are forged, where professional networks are built, where a sense of shared identity is cultivated. The traditional degree is a powerful signal of this social capital. It is a signal that a person has been a member of a particular community, that they have been vetted by that community, and that they have access to the resources and the network of that community. This is not a bug to be fixed; it is a feature to be celebrated. It is a recognition of the fact that success in the modern economy is not just about what you know; it is about who you know. The purely individualistic, atomized, and radically meritocratic vision of the evidence-based credential is a vision that ignores the social nature of work and the importance of social capital in a complex and interconnected world.

**3. The Argument from the Signaling Power of Scarcity: The Degree as a Positional Good**

The value of a credential is not just a function of the skills it represents; it is also a function of its scarcity. An elite degree is valuable not just because it signals a certain level of academic achievement, but because it is scarce. It is a positional good, a good whose value is derived from the fact that not everyone can have it. This is not a cynical observation; it is a fundamental feature of human social life. We are social creatures, and we are constantly looking for ways to signal our status and our position in the social hierarchy. The traditional degree, with its clear and well-established hierarchy of prestige, is a powerful tool for this kind of social signaling. The world of evidence-based credentials, with its proliferation of micro-credentials and its lack of a clear and stable hierarchy, is a world of signal confusion, a world where it is much harder to know who is good and who is not. In this view, the attempt to create a purely meritocratic system of credentialing is a fool's errand, an attempt to repeal the laws of social gravity.

**4. The Argument from the Limits of Evidence: The Degree as a Bet on Potential**

An evidence-based credential is a record of what a person has done in the past. A traditional degree is a bet on what a person can do in the future. When an employer hires a graduate from a top university, they are not just hiring a set of skills; they are hiring a person who has been selected for their raw intellectual horsepower, their work ethic, and their potential to learn and grow. The degree is a signal of that potential. It is a signal that a person has the ability to tackle new and unfamiliar challenges, to learn new skills, and to adapt to a changing world. An evidence-based credential, with its focus on the specific skills that a person has already mastered, is a much more narrow and backward-looking signal. In this view, the obsession with evidence is a form of risk aversion, a form of risk aversion that would lead employers to hire for the skills of today, rather than for the potential of tomorrow.

These are not arguments for a return to a world where the only thing that matters is the name on your diploma. They are arguments for a more humble and more realistic view of the limits of credentialing, a view that recognizes that the traditional degree, for all its flaws, is a rich, multi-faceted, and deeply human signal that captures a range of qualities that are essential for success in the modern economy.


## IBM: A Case Study in Building a Skills-Based Organization

For over a century, IBM has been a leader in technological innovation. But in the early 2010s, the company was facing a new and unprecedented challenge: the rapid pace of technological change was creating a massive skills gap, and the traditional, degree-based approach to talent management was no longer sufficient to meet the needs of a rapidly evolving business. In response, IBM launched one of the most ambitious and successful digital credentialing programs in the world, a program that has become a model for how large enterprises can use evidence-based credentials to build a more agile, more skilled, and more diverse workforce.

**The Problem: A Static Model for a Dynamic World**

Like most large enterprises, IBM's talent management system was built around a set of traditional, static, and largely backward-looking signals. The university degree was the primary currency of the realm, and career progression was often based on seniority and time-in-seat. This model was ill-suited to the needs of a company that was in the midst of a massive transformation, a company that was moving from a focus on hardware and software to a focus on cloud, AI, and services. The skills that had made IBM successful in the past were not the skills that would make it successful in the future, and the company needed a new way to identify, to cultivate, and to signal the skills that mattered most.

**The Solution: A Global Program for Verifiable, Evidence-Based Credentials**

In 2014, IBM launched its digital badge program, a global initiative designed to create a new, more dynamic, and more transparent currency for skills. The program was built on a set of core principles:

1.  **A Focus on Evidence, Not Just Completion:** An IBM badge is not just a participation trophy. It is a verifiable, evidence-based credential that is earned by passing a rigorous, performance-based assessment. Every badge is backed by a portfolio of evidence that shows exactly what the earner can do.

2.  **A Commitment to Open Standards:** IBM was a founding member of the Open Badge standard, and the company has been a leader in the development of a common, interoperable infrastructure for digital credentials. This commitment to open standards has been critical to the success of the program, as it has allowed IBM badges to be easily shared and verified across a wide range of platforms.

3.  **A Deep Partnership with the Business:** The IBM badge program is not an HR initiative. It is a business initiative. Every badge is co-created with the business units to ensure that it is aligned with the skills that are most critical to the success of the company. This has ensured that the badges are not just a nice-to-have, but a must-have for employees who want to advance their careers at IBM.

**The Results: A More Skilled, More Engaged, and More Diverse Workforce**

The results of IBM's digital credentialing program have been transformative. The company has issued over 2 million badges to over 600,000 employees, and the program has had a profound impact on the way that IBM hires, develops, and promotes talent. Here are some of the key results:

-   **A More Skilled Workforce:** The badge program has created a new culture of continuous learning at IBM, and it has provided employees with a clear and transparent path to upskill and reskill for the jobs of the future. The company has seen a significant increase in the number of employees with in-demand skills in areas like AI, cloud, and cybersecurity.

-   **A More Engaged Workforce:** The badge program has been a powerful tool for employee engagement and retention. Employees who earn badges are more likely to be engaged in their work, more likely to feel a sense of accomplishment, and more likely to stay with the company.

-   **A More Diverse Workforce:** The badge program has been a powerful tool for promoting diversity and inclusion. By focusing on skills, not just degrees, the program has created new pathways to opportunity for individuals from non-traditional backgrounds. IBM has found that employees who earn badges are more likely to be promoted, and that the program has been particularly beneficial for women and underrepresented minorities.

**The Lesson for the Future of Credentialing:**

IBM's story is a powerful illustration of the transformative potential of evidence-based credentials. It is a story of how a large, complex, and traditional organization was able to build a new, more agile, and more meritocratic system for talent management. It is a story that proves that the future of credentialing is not about more badges, but about more evidence. It is about creating a world where what you can prove you can do is more important than where you went to school, a world where every individual has the opportunity to build a rich and verifiable record of their skills, and to unlock their full potential.
