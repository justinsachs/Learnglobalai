# Bloom's 2 Sigma Problem — Fact-Check Notes

## Source: Education Next (2024) — Paul T. von Hippel
URL: https://www.educationnext.org/two-sigma-tutoring-separating-science-fiction-from-science-fact/

## Key Findings for Manuscript Revision

### What the evidence ACTUALLY shows:
- The 2-sigma effect was real in the original 1980s studies (Anania and Burke dissertations)
- BUT it was obtained on a **narrow, specialized test** — not broad achievement tests
- The effect was NOT from tutoring alone — it was a "potent cocktail" of interventions:
  - Tutoring
  - Training and coaching in effective instructional practices
  - Extra time
  - Frequent testing, feedback, and retesting
- The 2-sigma effect has **never been replicated** in rigorous evaluations
- Typical effect of well-designed tutoring programs: **~0.33 standard deviations** (about 1/3 sigma, not 2)
- Tutoring effects range from 2 full standard deviations DOWN TO ZERO OR WORSE

### What this means for our manuscript:
- We CANNOT state "Bloom's 2 Sigma Problem proves tutoring improves outcomes by 2 standard deviations" as a universal fact
- We SHOULD say: "Bloom's 1984 research suggested that one-on-one tutoring combined with mastery learning could raise achievement by up to two standard deviations under specific conditions — a finding that has inspired decades of research even as subsequent studies have found more modest but still significant effects."
- We SHOULD acknowledge the limitations: "While the full 2-sigma effect has proven difficult to replicate at scale, the underlying principle — that personalized feedback dramatically outperforms mass instruction — is supported by a substantial body of evidence."

## Correct Citation:
Bloom, B.S. (1984). The 2 Sigma Problem: The Search for Methods of Group Instruction as Effective as One-to-One Tutoring. *Educational Researcher*, 13(6), 4-16.

## Supporting Evidence (What We CAN Cite):
- Meta-analysis of 36 mastery learning studies: average effect size of 0.59 (PMC, 2022, cited by 144)
- Hattie's Visible Learning: mastery learning effect size 0.57
- Tutoring meta-analyses: median effect size 0.36-0.40 across rigorous studies
- AI tutoring (Brookings, 2024): modest but real positive effects in controlled studies

## Recommended Manuscript Language:
"Benjamin Bloom's landmark 1984 study suggested that one-on-one tutoring combined with mastery learning could raise student achievement by as much as two standard deviations above the classroom average — a finding so striking it became known as the '2 Sigma Problem.' While subsequent research has found that the full effect is difficult to replicate outside the original experimental conditions, the core insight endures: personalized, feedback-rich instruction consistently outperforms standardized mass delivery. Meta-analyses of mastery learning programs find average effect sizes of 0.57-0.59, placing it among the most effective instructional interventions ever studied."
