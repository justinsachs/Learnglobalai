# Assessment Integrity, Cheating, and Proctoring Research

## Integrity by Design

### University of Oregon

**Key Strategies:**
1.  **Align Assessments with Learning Outcomes:** Ensure assessments measure what you want students to learn.
2.  **Use Authentic Assessments:** Design tasks that mirror real-world challenges.
3.  **Scaffold Major Assignments:** Break large assignments into smaller, manageable steps.
4.  **Promote a Culture of Integrity:** Be explicit about expectations and the value of academic integrity.

**Source:** University of Oregon. "Designing Assessments for Academic Integrity." https://teaching.uoregon.edu/resources/designing-assessments-academic-integrity

### Monash University

**Key Insight:** "Assessment shows what we value and directs student learning. Assessing low level cognitive skills and memorisation lends itself more to cheating."

**Implication:** If you assess memorization, you invite cheating. If you assess higher-order thinking, you make cheating harder and less useful.

**Source:** Monash University. "Design assessment for academic integrity." https://www.monash.edu/learning-teaching/teachhq/Assessment/academic-integrity/how-to/design-assessment-for-academic-integrity

### Turnitin (November 2025)

**Key Shift:** "A new focus for assessment design: **Integrity, not just detection**."

**Implication:** The goal is to design assessments that are inherently resistant to cheating, rather than just trying to catch cheaters after the fact.

**Source:** Turnitin (November 20, 2025). "A new focus for assessment design: Integrity, not just detection." https://www.turnitin.com/blog/a-new-focus-for-assessment-design-integrity-not-just-detection

## Cheating in Online Exams

### Springer (August 2023) - Newton

**Key Statistic:** "Online exam cheating was self-reported by a substantial minority (**44.7%**) of students in total. Pre-COVID this was 29.9%..."

**Citation Count:** Cited by 187 papers.

**Implication:** Cheating is rampant in online exams, and the problem has gotten worse since the pandemic.

**Citation:** Newton, P.M. (2024). "How Common is Cheating in Online Exams and did it Increase During COVID-19?" Springer. [Cited by 187 papers] https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10805-023-09485-5

### MeritTrac (September 2025)

**Key Statistic:** "Recent studies reveal that **64% of students admit to some form of online exam cheating**, compared to 43% in traditional classroom settings."

**Implication:** The online environment makes cheating easier and more common.

**Source:** MeritTrac (September 17, 2025). "Cheating in Online Exams: How AI and Technology Are Fighting Back." https://merittrac.com/blogs/cheating-in-online-exams-a-growing-threat-and-how-technology-is-fighting-back/

### Inside Higher Ed (April 2022)

**Study Finding:** A study by an online proctoring service found a **6.6% rate of cheating**, up 14 times from before the pandemic.

**Implication:** While self-reported rates are high, even observed rates show a massive increase in cheating.

**Source:** Inside Higher Ed (April 27, 2022). "Study: online exam cheating is up." https://www.insidehighered.com/news/2022/04/28/study-online-exam-cheating

## Remote Proctoring Technologies

### MIT Technology Review (August 2020)

**Key Criticism:** "Algorithmic proctoring is a modern surveillance technology that **reinforces white supremacy, sexism, ableism, and transphobia**."

**Implication:** These systems are not neutral. They are biased against non-normative bodies and behaviors.

**Source:** MIT Technology Review (August 7, 2020). "Software that monitors students during tests perpetuates inequality and violates privacy." https://www.technologyreview.com/2020/08/07/1006132/software-algorithms-proctoring-online-tests-ai-ethics/

### PMC (2021) - Coghlan et al.

**Key Concern:** "Such technologies have alarmed some students who see them as a **‘Big Brother-like’ threat to liberty and privacy**, and as potentially unfair and discriminatory."

**Citation Count:** Cited by 309 papers.

**Implication:** The use of remote proctoring is highly controversial and can damage trust between students and institutions.

**Citation:** Coghlan, S., et al. (2021). "Good Proctor or ‘Big Brother’? Ethics of Online Exam Supervision Technologies." PMC. [Cited by 309 papers] https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8407138/

### BCcampus (February 2026)

**Key Argument:** "Remote proctoring is not ed tech. It’s **academic surveillance software** designed to monitor and control student behaviour during exams."

**Implication:** The framing of this technology is important. It is not a neutral tool for learning; it is a tool for surveillance.

**Source:** BCcampus (February 6, 2026). "Remote Proctoring Through an Ethical Lens: The Case Against Surveillance." https://bccampus.ca/2026/02/06/remote-proctoring-through-an-ethical-lens-the-case-against-surveillance/

## Key Takeaways for Book

### The Problem: Cheating Is Rampant

1.  **High Rates:** As many as 64% of students admit to cheating in online exams.
2.  **Increased Since COVID:** The shift to online learning has exacerbated the problem, with cheating rates increasing significantly.

### The Wrong Solution: Surveillance

3.  **Remote Proctoring is Surveillance:** These technologies are not neutral ed tech; they are academic surveillance systems.
4.  **Biased and Unfair:** Algorithmic proctoring systems are known to be biased against non-normative bodies and behaviors.
5.  **Damages Trust:** The use of surveillance technologies can create a climate of suspicion and damage the relationship between students and institutions.

### The Right Solution: Integrity by Design

6.  **Prevention, Not Detection:** The goal is to design assessments that are inherently resistant to cheating, not just to catch cheaters.
7.  **Assess Higher-Order Thinking:** If you assess memorization, you invite cheating. If you assess application, analysis, and creation, you make cheating harder and less useful.
8.  **Authentic Assessments:** Design tasks that mirror real-world challenges. It is hard to cheat on a task that requires you to produce a unique, complex artifact.
9.  **Process, Not Product:** Assess the process, not just the final product. Use scaffolding, check-ins, and oral defenses to ensure the work is the student's own.
10. **AI as a Partner, Not an Adversary:** Instead of trying to block AI, design assessments that require students to use AI as a tool and then critically evaluate the output.

### Strategic Implications

11. **The Arms Race Is a Losing Battle:** Trying to build a better mousetrap to catch cheaters is a losing game. Students will always find new ways to cheat.
12. **Assessment Drives Learning:** The design of your assessments signals what you value. If you value integrity, you must design for it.
13. **Integrity Is an Outcome, Not a Constraint:** Academic integrity is not something you bolt on at the end. It is an outcome of a well-designed learning experience.
