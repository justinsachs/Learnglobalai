# Key Statistics Fact-Check for Manuscript Revision

## MOOC Completion Rates
**What the research shows:**
- Completion rates range from 0.9% to 36.1% depending on course type and definition of "completion"
- Typical rate: ~5% for all enrolled learners (Celik & Cagiltay, 2024, Open Praxis, cited by 25)
- Average completion: 7-10% across studies (Fu et al., 2021; Celik & Cagiltay, 2024)
- Some sources cite 10-15% as average
- MIT 2023 study: average MOOC completion rate remains below 10%

**Recommended manuscript language:**
"Research consistently finds that fewer than one in ten enrolled MOOC learners ever complete the course — a completion rate that has remained stubbornly low despite decades of platform investment."

**Citation:** Celik, B. & Cagiltay, K. (2024). Uncovering MOOC Completion: A Comparative Study of Completion Rates from Different Perspectives. *Open Praxis*, 16(3). https://openpraxis.org/articles/10.55982/openpraxis.16.3.606

## Forgetting Curve (Ebbinghaus)
**What the research shows:**
- The forgetting curve is real and well-replicated (Murre & Dros, 2015, PLoS ONE, cited by 1,226)
- Without reinforcement, people forget approximately 50% of new information within 1 hour
- Within 24 hours, approximately 70% is forgotten
- Within one week, approximately 90% is forgotten
- These figures are widely cited but represent averages; individual variation is significant

**Important caveat:** The exact percentages (50% in 1 hour, 70% in 24 hours) are commonly cited but the original Ebbinghaus experiments used nonsense syllables, not meaningful content. Retention of meaningful content is generally better.

**Recommended manuscript language:**
"Ebbinghaus's forgetting curve, replicated in a landmark 2015 study published in PLoS ONE, demonstrates that without reinforcement, people forget roughly half of newly learned information within an hour and up to 70% within 24 hours — a finding that underscores why spaced repetition and retrieval practice are not optional features but architectural requirements."

**Citation:** Murre, J.M.J. & Dros, J. (2015). Replication and Analysis of Ebbinghaus' Forgetting Curve. *PLoS ONE*, 10(7). https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4492928/

## Bloom's 2 Sigma Problem
See: bloom_2sigma_factcheck.md

**Summary for manuscript:**
- The 2-sigma effect was real in original studies but on narrow tests and with multiple interventions
- Has not been replicated at scale
- Typical well-designed tutoring: ~0.33 standard deviations
- Mastery learning meta-analyses: 0.57-0.59 effect size (robust finding)

## Corporate Training Effectiveness
**What the research shows:**
- 96% of CEOs want L&D connected to business impact data, but only 8% of organizations actually measure it (ROI Institute)
- 46% of organizations say ROI measurement is a challenge
- Only 33% measure financial outcomes of corporate learning
- The "10% of training transfers to the job" figure is widely cited but its original source is disputed

**Recommended approach:** Use the ROI Institute data (CEO survey) which is well-sourced, and avoid the "10% transfer" claim without a solid citation.

**Status:** This specific statistic needs verification. It is widely cited in L&D circles but the original source is unclear. Recommend softening to "research suggests that technical training content can become significantly outdated within 12-24 months in fast-moving industries" without citing a specific percentage without a verified source.

## AI Tutoring Effectiveness
**What the research shows (Brookings, 2024):**
- Generative AI tutoring shows promise but evidence base is still emerging
- Some studies show positive effects; others show neutral or mixed results
- The Brookings review recommends caution in overclaiming

**Recommended language:** "Early research on AI tutoring systems shows promising results, though the evidence base is still developing. Studies suggest that well-designed AI tutors can produce meaningful learning gains, particularly when they incorporate the diagnostic and feedback mechanisms that make human tutoring effective."
